A Pitch Document

The Woodstock Plan

Saturday Residency at Stauffer Chiropractic

A 60-day trial toward a sustainable local partnership between independent practitioners refusing the rollup.

One Principle

Every clause in this plan answers the same test: does it make all three parties want the same outcome on a typical Saturday?

If a clause makes one party root against another's success, it's the wrong clause. Hold this throughline as the deal evolves.

Two practitioners pooling labor in a single location to refuse the private-equity rollup eating allied health is, materially, a small act of organizing — the kind that keeps independent practice viable in markets where the chains have most of the real estate.

The Deal in Three Lines

For every session billed through the residency, until the monthly cap fills:

50%

To the practicing therapist

No chain-shop deductions. No quotas. Their book, their share.

45%

To Stauffer Chiropractic

Premises, booking infrastructure, patient pipeline. Capped at $1,800/mo — sized to cover the practice rent.

5%

To Alex as program head

Recruiting, training, scheduling, supply management, holding the standard. The structural cost of running a residency that runs itself.

Three lines. Add to 100. No skim. No routing. No surprises.

The Cap

Your rent, every month. Predictable. Protected.

$1,800

Per month — aggregate, residency-wide

Sized to the rent

Confirmed at $1,800/mo. Cap math is exact, no resize needed.

Aggregate, not per-seat

All three therapists feed the same pool. Lopsided months still fill the cap.

Risk distributed

No single therapist carries a third of your nut. The team carries it together.

"If T2 catches the flu in February, T3 and Alex still feed the cap. You're more protected, not less."

We All Uncap Together

Once the team has paid your $1,800 for the month, every additional session that month splits differently.

80%

Practicing therapist (above cap)

Their 50% plus 30% of the freed share. Recruiting unlock — every chiro 1099 in the metro tops out lower.

20%

Alex (above cap)

5% program head + 15% organizer cut. The 15% only triggers when the team has earned it — pay-for-performance, not paid for nothing.

$1,800

Stauffer (cap protected)

Your number is locked at $1,800 for the month — overflow does not eat into it, does not change it. Predictability is the deal.

Every session anyone books fills the same pool. When the pool tips, everyone's overflow rate kicks in. Three therapists rowing together hit the uncap faster than three therapists rowing separately. The structure makes us teammates by mechanic, not by speech.

How It Works on Saturdays

1

60-Day Solo Trial

Alex only — no second or third therapist yet. Confirms the model works in this room with this front desk before adding contractors. Both parties revisit terms in good faith at day 60.

2

Add Therapists Two and Three

Each contracts directly with Stauffer Chiropractic, not as Alex's subcontractor — clean legal/insurance structure. Alex's role is program head: recruiting, onboarding, scheduling discipline, technique standards. Each therapist carries their own malpractice insurance.

3

Booking Through the Practice

Stauffer's existing patient intake / scheduling and POS handle the residency. Revenue base for the split is net of merchant processing fees — fair to Stauffer (he eats the processing cost), fair to therapists (same net number applies to everyone).

4

Tips Are 100% Therapist

Tips bypass the practice POS entirely. Each therapist runs their own Venmo / CashApp / Zelle QR or personal Square reader. Cleaner books for the practice, cleaner classification for the therapist.

5

Renegotiate at Day 60, Then Yearly

14-day notice if either party walks. Renegotiation triggers — revenue floor, scheduling conflicts, anything either party flags — written into the one-pager so neither side is stuck guessing.

Why This Exists

Both parties to this deal are independents resisting the private-equity rollup pattern eating allied health: Massage Envy for MTs, The Joint for chiros, Aspen Dental for dentists, VCA for vets. Same playbook everywhere — buy up the locals, squeeze the labor, charge the customer more.

Two practitioners pooling labor in a single location to refuse the rollup is, materially, a small act of organizing — the kind that keeps independent practice viable in markets where the chains have most of the real estate.

A defined portion of the program-head share funds People's Elbow mutual aid operations — 50/50 fundraiser events, free community demonstrations, the chair-massage outreach the Saturday gig grew from. The residency only earns its name if it feeds the practice it grew from.

Same Outcome. Different Roles.

Doc gets his nut. Always. Sized to the rent, capped to the dollar, distributed across the team so no single therapist carries the load.

Therapists get a 50% headline that climbs to 80% on overflow — a number no chiro 1099 in the metro touches.

The program-head share earns 5% on coordination labor, 20% only when the team has demolished the cap. Pay-for-performance, not paid for nothing.

We all uncap together.
